Surviving Your First Year of Graduate School: Lessons Learned

Looking back on my PhD, here is the practical advice I wish someone had given me before my first year—on research, coursework, wellbeing, and community.

Introduction

Now that I’ve finished my PhD, I find myself looking back on that first year with a lot more perspective than I had while living through it. It was exciting, challenging, and often overwhelming—a year of transition that doubled as a year of self-doubt.

These are the lessons I wish someone had handed me before I started. None of them are about getting perfect grades or publishing early. They’re about building a foundation, protecting your wellbeing, and setting yourself up for the long haul.

Think Slow, Act Fast

Some of the best advice I came across in grad school comes from How Big Things Get Done by Bent Flyvbjerg and Dan Gardner: “think slow, act fast.” Before diving into experiments, code, or data, spend real time understanding the landscape of your field.

Literature search isn’t a formality—it’s one of the most important skills you’ll build. Reading broadly helps you avoid reinventing the wheel, spot the gaps where your work can matter, and develop a sense for what separates good research from forgettable research.

A few things that worked for me:

  • Start broad, then narrow: Begin with review papers and recent high-impact work, then drill into the specifics.
  • Read with a system: For each paper, note the problem, the approach, the limitations, and the open questions.
  • Lean on your advisor and labmates: Ask them which papers are foundational and which are worth your time.
  • Don’t try to read everything: You can’t. Aim to understand the key ideas and the current state of the field.

Thinking slow doesn’t mean being unproductive—it means being intentional about where you spend your energy.

Don’t Get Overwhelmed by Research Progress

In my first year I felt constant pressure to produce. Other students seemed to be presenting at conferences and publishing, and I wondered why I wasn’t. In hindsight, that pressure was almost entirely self-inflicted.

Your first year isn’t about research output. It’s about learning to think. Use the time to explore different directions before committing, ask the big questions in your field, and build the tools and skills your research will eventually demand.

Research is uncertain and nonlinear. You’ll have stretches of fast progress and stretches where nothing works—both are normal. Don’t compare your chapter 1 to someone else’s chapter 10. Focus instead on learning to think like a researcher, building relationships with your advisor and labmates, and developing habits that will carry you through the years ahead.

Use Coursework, but Keep the Big Picture

First-year coursework can feel disconnected from your research, but it’s doing more than you think. It builds the theoretical foundation your research will rest on, exposes you to areas you didn’t know you cared about, and sharpens how you think.

While you’re in it, keep asking how each course connects to your interests, what transferable skills you’re picking up, and what new questions it raises—good courses tend to raise more than they answer.

And give yourself permission to prioritize coursework early on. The goal is to do well in your courses while beginning to explore research—not to excel at both at once.

Prioritize Your Health and Wellbeing

Grad school is a marathon, not a sprint. Your health isn’t negotiable—it’s the thing that makes everything else possible.

  • Sleep and move: You can’t do your best work exhausted, and regular exercise did more for my stress levels than almost anything else.
  • Eat like you mean it: It’s easy to skip meals when you’re busy, but your brain needs fuel.
  • Mind your mental health: Learn to recognize when you’re overwhelmed, and use your university’s counseling services without hesitation—therapy is a tool, not a weakness.
  • Set boundaries: Learn to say no, take real weekends, and don’t check email constantly. Overcommitting is the fastest road to burnout.

Taking care of yourself isn’t selfish. It’s a prerequisite for doing good work.

Build Community

Grad school can be isolating, especially if you’ve moved somewhere new. Building community turned out to be one of the best investments I made.

The reasons are simple: you need people who understand the ups and downs, many of the best ideas come from casual conversations, and the people around you become your professional network and your friends.

What helped:

  • Show up: Go to seminars, social events, and department gatherings.
  • Be proactive: Don’t wait for invitations—organize the coffee, the study group, the dinner.
  • Get to know your lab: Some of my closest friendships started a few desks away.
  • Build relationships with faculty too: Visit office hours just to talk, ask about their research, and seek mentorship beyond your advisor.

Relationships take time. Be patient and keep showing up.

Explore Your New Home

If you moved for grad school, take time to get to know your new city. This isn’t a distraction from your work—it’s an investment in your happiness.

Exploring eased my homesickness, gave me places and activities to look forward to, and made the whole experience more memorable than just the research. Walk or bike your neighborhood, find your favorite cafe, get outside, go to local events, and take the occasional day trip. A happy, well-rounded person makes a better researcher.

Dealing with Impostor Syndrome

Almost everyone in grad school feels like an impostor at some point—like they don’t belong, aren’t smart enough, or will eventually be “found out.” I certainly did.

It’s common for understandable reasons: you’re surrounded by brilliant people, the standards are high, research is inherently uncertain, and comparison is effortless. What helped me cope:

  • Talk about it: Naming the feeling to friends or a counselor takes away most of its power—and you’ll find you’re far from alone.
  • Reframe it: “I don’t belong here” becomes “I’m learning, and that’s the point.”
  • Track your own growth: Compare yourself to where you were, not to everyone else.
  • Celebrate small wins and remember you were admitted for a reason.

The people who look the most confident are often struggling too—they’ve just gotten better at managing it. Feeling like an impostor doesn’t make you one. It means you’re somewhere hard, learning and growing.

Conclusion

The first year of grad school is a year of transition and growth, and it’s completely normal to feel overwhelmed or unsure of what you’re doing. If I could distill what I learned, it would be this:

  • Think slow, act fast. Understand your field before you dive in.
  • Don’t chase output too early. The first year is for building a foundation.
  • Use coursework, but keep the big picture in view.
  • Protect your health. It’s the engine for everything else.
  • Build community, and explore where you live.
  • Expect impostor syndrome, and remember almost everyone shares it.

Grad school is a journey, not a destination, and the first year is where the foundation gets laid—academically, professionally, and personally. Be patient with yourself. You’re more capable than you feel.

Good luck, and enjoy the ride.
